Basis for a Topology

A basis for a topology on a set \( X \) is a collection \( \mathcal{B} \) of subsets of \( X \) (called basis elements) such that:

  1. For each \( x \in X \), there is at least one basis element \( B \in \mathcal{B} \) containing \( x \).
  2. If \( x \) belongs to the intersection of two basis elements \( B_1 \) and \( B_2 \), then there is a basis element \( B_3 \) in \( \mathcal{B} \) such that \( x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2 \).

The topology \( \tau \) generated by the basis \( \mathcal{B} \) is defined as follows: a subset \( U \subseteq X \) is open in \( \tau \) if for every \( x \in U \), there is a basis element \( B \in \mathcal{B} \) such that \( x \in B \subseteq U \).

Examples

Example 1: Standard Topology on \( \mathbb{R} \)

Consider the set of all open intervals \( (a, b) \) where \( a, b \in \mathbb{R} \). This collection forms a basis for the standard topology on \( \mathbb{R} \).

Verification:

  • For each \( x \in \mathbb{R} \), the interval \( (x-1, x+1) \) contains \( x \), so every point in \( \mathbb{R} \) is contained in some basis element.
  • If \( x \in (a_1, b_1) \cap (a_2, b_2) \), then \( x \) is in the intersection of two open intervals. We can find another open interval \( (a_3, b_3) \) such that \( x \in (a_3, b_3) \subseteq (a_1, b_1) \cap (a_2, b_2) \).

Example 2: Discrete Topology

Let \( X \) be any set. The collection of all singletons \( \{ \{ x \} \mid x \in X \} \) forms a basis for the discrete topology on \( X \).

Verification:

  • For each \( x \in X \), the singleton \( \{ x \} \) contains \( x \), so every point in \( X \) is contained in some basis element.
  • The intersection of any two singletons is either empty or another singleton, satisfying the second condition trivially.

Example 3: Lower Limit Topology on \( \mathbb{R} \)

Consider the set of all half-open intervals \( [a, b) \) where \( a, b \in \mathbb{R} \). This collection forms a basis for the lower limit topology on \( \mathbb{R} \).

Verification:

  • For each \( x \in \mathbb{R} \), the interval \( [x, x+1) \) contains \( x \), so every point in \( \mathbb{R} \) is contained in some basis element.
  • If \( x \in [a_1, b_1) \cap [a_2, b_2) \), then \( x \) is in the intersection of two half-open intervals. We can find another half-open interval \( [a_3, b_3) \) such that \( x \in [a_3, b_3) \subseteq [a_1, b_1) \cap [a_2, b_2) \).
